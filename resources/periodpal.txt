HOW TO MAKE REUSABLE PADS USING CLOTH OR SAFE MATERIALS
When you can't afford to buy pads every month, you don't have to suffer in silence. You can make your own reusable pads using clean, safe materials just make sure they're soft, absorbent, and comfortable.

ðŸ§µ MATERIALS YOU WILL NEED:
- Clean cotton fabric (from old T-shirts, bedsheets, or wrappers)
- Towel material or thick cotton cloth (for the absorbent middle layer)
- Needle and thread or sewing machine
- Scissors
- Press buttons, snaps, or safety pins
- Optional: waterproof material like nylon or plastic from raincoats

âœ‚ï¸ STEPS TO MAKE YOUR REUSABLE PAD

1. Draw and Cut the Shape
- Place a pad or folded cloth on paper and trace its shape
- Cut two identical pieces from cotton fabric (top and bottom layers)
- Cut one or two pieces from towel material (middle absorption layer)

2. Layer It Up
- Bottom cotton layer
- Absorbent layers (towel material) in the middle
- Top cotton layer
- Optional: waterproof material beneath bottom layer

- Dry under direct sunlight in a clean, airy place
- Store in a dry, clean bag or container until next use
- One reusable pad can last 2-3 years with proper care

PERIOD DELAY 101

ðŸŒ¸ WHY MENSTRUATION SOMETIMES COMES LATE

Your menstrual cycle isn't a robotâ€”it's more like a rhythm your body dances to. Sometimes the rhythm changes, and that's okay.

ðŸŒ¦ï¸ 1. Stress (Emotional or Physical)
When you're worried, anxious, or overwhelmed, your body produces cortisol (stress hormone).
Cortisol can confuse the hypothalamus (the part controlling your period), delaying ovulation.
Example: During exam week or after conflict, your body might delay menstruation.

ðŸ¥— 2. Poor Nutrition or Sudden Weight Changes
Your body needs energy and nutrients for a healthy cycle.
Sudden weight loss/gain, skipping meals, or eating too little signals "famine mode."
Your body may delay menstruation to conserve energy.
Example: Crash diets or skipping meals throw hormones off balance.

ðŸƒâ€â™€ï¸ 3. Excessive Exercise
Exercise is amazingâ€”but too much without rest or food can stop your period.
Your body uses most energy to repair muscles instead of maintaining reproductive cycle.
Called Amenorrhea; reversible when body gets rest and food.

âš–ï¸ 4. Hormonal Imbalances (Especially PCOS)
Polycystic Ovary Syndrome makes your body produce extra male hormones (testosterone).
This affects ovulation and makes periods come late or not at all.
Signs: Irregular periods, acne, extra body hair, weight gain around tummy.
It can be managed with proper care and lifestyle changes.

ðŸŒ¡ï¸ 5. Illness or Infection
Short-term illnesses (flu, malaria, typhoid) cause temporary hormonal changes.
Delays are usually temporary; cycle resets once you recover.

ðŸ•¯ï¸ 6. Change in Routine or Environment
Travel, relocation, lack of sleep, or shift in daily habits throws off circadian rhythm.
Your period might skip a few days or even a week.
Example: Travel to different time zone or moving schools.

ðŸ’Š 7. Birth Control or Medications
Starting/stopping birth control pills, injections, or implants causes delays as body adjusts.
Other medicines (depression meds, thyroid meds) also affect cycle.
Usually balances out after 1-3 months.

â³ 8. Puberty or Perimenopause
First period: body is still figuring things out; normal for cycles to be irregular for 1-2 years.
Later in life: approaching menopause, periods become irregular again.
Example: 13-year-old might skip a month then have heavy flow nextâ€”part of body learning.

ðŸ‘§ 9. Nutrient Deficiencies
Low iron, vitamin D, or B vitamins interfere with hormone production.
These nutrients control signals telling body when to release eggs.
Tip: Eat balanced mealsâ€”fruits, vegetables, beans, eggs, fish, lots of water.

ðŸ˜´ 10. Sleep Problems
Poor sleep or staying up late affects melatonin, which disrupts estrogen/progesterone balance.
Tip: Get at least 7-8 hours of sleep daily.

ðŸ’• 11. Pregnancy
If unprotected sex occurred and period is late, pregnancy is one reason.
Body didn't shed lining because baby might be growing.
Note: Check safely with test or health worker if unsure. Better to know early than stress silently.

ðŸŒ» Key Takeaway
A late period doesn't always mean something is wrong. Sometimes it's just your body saying "I need a little break."

ðŸŒ¸ SANITARY PRODUCTS GUIDE

There are several types of menstrual products. Choose based on comfort, lifestyle, and access.

Types of Products:

1. Disposable Pads
- Pros: Easy, sanitary, convenient
- Cons: Need regular changing, disposal/waste issue
- Change every 4-6 hours

2. Tampons
- Pros: Discreet, good for swimming
- Cons: Risk of TSS if misused; change every 4-8 hours
- Warning: Tampons don't remove virginity

3. Menstrual Cups
- Pros: Reusable, eco-friendly, cost-saving
- Cons: Needs practice to insert/remove; cleaning required
- Can wear for 8-12 hours

4. Reusable Cloth Pads
- Pros: Cheap long-term, eco-friendly
- Cons: Needs washing and sun-drying

5. Period Underwear
- Pros: Comfortable, reusable
- Cons: Check absorbency level; often need backup

6. Pain Relief Meds & Heat Packs
- Not a product per se, but essential self-care tools

Hygiene Tips:
- Change pads/tampons every 4-6 hours (sooner if heavy)
- Clean reusable products properly
- Avoid douching
- Wash outer genital area with water and mild soap only

Disposal:
- Wrap used pads/tampons in paper and throw in bin
- Do NOT flush (plumbing and environment)
- Reusables: rinse, wash with soap, sun-dry, store dry

MYTHS vs TRUTH

Myth: Tampons remove virginity
Truth: Tampons don't remove virginity. Virginity is a social concept, not a medical state. Inserting a tampon is not sex.

Myth: Periods make you "dirty"
Truth: Menstruation is a natural biological process. The blood is clean and healthy. Periods don't make you unhygienic.

Myth: You can't get pregnant during your period
Truth: Pregnancy is possible during menstruation, especially with irregular cycles. Use protection if you don't want pregnancy.

Myth: You can't do anything during your period
Truth: You can exercise, work, study, play, and live fully during your period. Rest when needed, but don't stop living.

Myth: Period blood is different from regular blood
Truth: Menstrual blood contains blood, tissue from uterine lining, and cervical mucus. It's slightly different in composition but not "bad."

Myth: Heavy periods are normal
Truth: Heavy periods lasting 7+ days or requiring hourly pad changes may signal a condition. Consult a doctor if flow is unusually heavy.

Myth: Sex during period is "dirty"
Truth: You can have sex during your period. Use protection to prevent pregnancy and STIs. Some couples choose not to; that's personal preference.

Myth: Only women menstruate
Truth: Trans men and non-binary people also menstruate. Use inclusive language.

Myth: Periods are a punishment
Truth: Periods are a natural part of the reproductive cycle. They're not punishment; they're biology.

Myth: You should hide your period
Truth: Periods are normal. You don't need to hide pads, tampons, or talk about your cycle. Openness reduces stigma.

CYCLE TRACKING 101

Why Track Your Cycle?
- Predicts next period and fertile window
- Identifies patterns in mood, energy, symptoms
- Helps doctors diagnose issues
- Gives you body awareness and power

How to Track:
- Use notebook, calendar, or app
- Log:
  * First day of bleeding
  * Length of period
  * Flow heaviness
  * Symptoms (cramps, mood, energy)
  * Sexual activity (if relevant)
  * Medications
- Over 3-6 months, patterns emerge
- Average cycle is 21-35 days; yours may vary

What Normal Looks Like:
- Period length: 3-7 days
- Cycle length: 21-35 days
- Flow: light to heavy (manageable with products)

When to See a Doctor:
- Periods suddenly stop (not pregnant)
- Haven't started by age 16
- Extremely heavy bleeding (hourly pad changes)
- Bleeding >10 days
- Severe pain preventing daily life
- Fainting, dizziness, or signs of infection
- Cycles irregular for >1 year (unless just starting)
